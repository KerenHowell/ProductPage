Marie Curie

"The most inspirational woman in Science" - New Scientist

Marie Curie won the Nobel Prize twice, once for Chemistry and once for Physics. She was the first woman to win the Nobel Prize and remains the only woman to win it twice in different fields.

Early life

Maria Sklodowska was born in Warsaw, Poland, at that time part of the Russian Empire, on 7 November 1867. Her family had lost their property and fortunes due to their involvement in patriotic action aimed at securing Polish independence and Maria and her four siblings had to struggle to get ahead in life. As women, It was particularly difficult for Maria and her sisters .

Maria and her sister, Bronislawa, made an agreement that they would support each other financially to permit them to continue their education. Maria worked as a governess while Bronislawa studied medicine in Paris, then in 1891 Maria was able to join her sister and brother-in-law. Now known as Marie, she obtained her first degree in 1893, followed by a second degree in 1894.

Pierre Curie

It was while looking for laboratory space that she was introduced to Pierre Curie, who was able to find space for her to continue her work. eir mutual interest in science brought them together; they were married in July 1895. Marie’s dark-blue wedding costume later became her laboratory outfit. In 1897, their first daughter Irene was born. Marie’s second daughter, Eve was born. Marie was careful to ensure her daughters were aware of their Polish heritage, making sure that they spoke Polish and went on visits to Poland.

Research

Marie Curie was interested in the properties of then-newly discovered radiation and decided to research uranium rays as her thesis topic. She had determined that two uranium minerals, pitchblende and torbernite, were more active than uranium, therefore these minerals must contain small amounts of substances more active than uranium. S he set out to find them. Pierre, intrigued by her work, abandoned his own work on crystals to join her and after processing tonnes of pitchblende ore, in 1898, succeeded in demonstrating the existence of first polonium and then radium.

Awards and Honours

In June1903 she was awarded her doctorate and the Curies were invited to the Royal Institute in London to give a speech on radioactivity - a word which they had coned in the course of their work. As a woman, Marie was not permitted to speak so Pierre gave the address. In December of that year, the Curies shared the Nobel Prize for physics Henri Becquerel. Initially, Marie was not nominated- however, Pierre complained and Marie’s name was added.

Continuing work

In 1906, Pierre, who had been given a professorship and chair in physics by the University of Paris in 1905, was struck by a horse-drawn vehicle while crossing the road and died. The University offered Pierre’s post Marie, who accepted it, becoming the first female professor at the University of Paris.

Marie continued her research into radioactivity. During World War I, she developed mobile radiography units, bringing X-ray equipment to battlefield surgeons and training women as aides. She produced hollow needles containing radon (given off by radium) to be used for sterilising infected tissue. She was never recognised by the French government for her war effort.

Later life

After the war, Marie continued research into radium and radioactivity. The damaging effects of ionising radiation were not known at the time of her work and she had taken no safety measures - she had carried test tubes containing radioactive isotopes in her pocket and stored them in her desk drawer. Her papers from the 1890s are considered too dangerous to handle. Even her cookbooks are highly radioactive.Her papers are kept in lead-lined boxes, and those who wish to consult them must wear protective clothingShe was also exposed to unshielded X-rays during her work in the war, which is likely to have caused her death in 1934 from aplastic anaemia. She was 66 years old.

She is interred in the Pantheon, the first woman to so honoured on her own merits

Key dates

  • 1867 Maria Salomea Sklodowska born in Warsaw, Poland
  • 1891 Enrolled in the University of Paris, studying mathematics, chemistry and physics
  • 1893 Awarded her first degree in physics. She was awarded a second degree in 1894
  • 1895 Married French physicist Pierre Curie
  • 1898 Discovery of Polonium and Radium
  • 1903 Awarded doctorate by University of Paris. Awarded Nobel Prize in Physics ( jointly with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel)
  • 1906 Pierre Curie killed in a road accident
  • 1911 Awarded Nobel prize in Chemistry
  • 1934 Died, age 66, at Passy, Haut-Savoie, from aplastic anaemia
